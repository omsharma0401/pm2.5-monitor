Abstract

India's air quality crisis — responsible for over 1.67 million premature deaths annually — is exacerbated by a monitoring infrastructure concentrated in Tier-1 cities, leaving 70–90% data gaps in smaller urban centers. This study presents a dual-regime machine learning framework that fuses INSAT-3DS geostationary satellite Aerosol Optical Depth (AOD) with ERA5 meteorological reanalysis and CPCB ground observations to estimate hourly PM2.5 concentrations across nine Indian cities.

Two distinct models are evaluated: a mechanistic baseline relying on physical drivers (AOD, meteorology, temporal cycles) that achieves R² = 0.61, and an operational forecasting model incorporating temporal inertia that achieves R² = 0.92 (RMSE: 18.5 µg/m³). The framework reduces monitoring costs by 85–90% compared to ground networks and proposes a tiered deployment strategy for India's 4,000+ urban centers.

The Problem

A Monitoring Infrastructure Gap

India's Continuous Ambient Air Quality Monitoring Stations (CAAQMS) cost approximately ₹50 lakh to install and ₹10–15 lakh annually to maintain — a prohibitive expense that leaves most of the country without reliable data. A nation-wide ground network comparable to Tier-1 standards would require an estimated ₹2,600 crore in initial investment alone.

Existing ground stations typically exhibit 60–75% uptime, creating blind spots during critical pollution episodes. Tier-2 and Tier-3 cities experience 70–90% data gaps due to power interruptions, calibration drift, and vandalism.

Furthermore, point-source ground measurements fail to capture spatial heterogeneity across large urban agglomerations — a gap that satellite coverage can uniquely fill.

Methodology

A Dual-Regime Framework

Three data streams are fused at hourly resolution across nine cities spanning India's diverse climatic zones — monsoon coast, arid inland, and humid subtropical.

INSAT-3DS Satellite AOD

Level-2 Gridded AOD (V02R00) in HDF5 format retrieved from MOSDAC via a Selenium browser automation pipeline. 1,505 granules acquired over the study period. Available only during clear-sky daytime windows (11:15–14:15 IST), resulting in 93% temporal sparsity.

ERA5 Meteorological Reanalysis

0.25°×0.25° resolution fields from ECMWF covering Boundary Layer Height (BLH), temperature, dewpoint, surface pressure, and wind speed. ERA5 provides continuous all-weather coverage, filling temporal gaps when satellite AOD is unavailable.

CPCB Ground Observations

Hourly PM2.5 from Continuous Ambient Air Quality Monitoring Stations within each city. Quality-controlled with hard filters (0–1,000 µg/m³) and city-balanced at 42,426 records per city to prevent model bias toward data-rich regions like Delhi.

XGBoost with Optuna Hyperparameter Optimization

GPU-accelerated gradient boosting (tree_method='hist', device='cuda') with hyperparameters tuned by Optuna to minimize RMSE. Two configurations evaluated: Model B with temporal lag (pm25_lag1) for operational forecasting, and Model B-NoLag isolating physical signal from satellite and meteorology alone.

Results

Two Models, Two Purposes

Operational
Model B — With Temporal Lag
0.9259 R² (Test Set)
18.47 RMSE (µg/m³)

Incorporates the previous hour's PM2.5 measurement (pm25_lag1) to capture temporal inertia — the dominant signal in pollution time series. Best for hourly operational forecasting and public health alert systems.

SHAP analysis confirms pm25_lag1 contributes ~10× more than all other features combined (mean |SHAP| = 49.23). The model learns statistical persistence, not causal atmospheric physics.
Physical Baseline
Model B-NoLag — Physical Drivers Only
0.6148 R² (Test Set)
51.11 RMSE (µg/m³)

Excludes historical observations — relying solely on satellite AOD and ERA5 meteorology. This configuration is deployable in cities with no existing ground infrastructure and remains interpretable for policy analysis.

Relative Humidity is the dominant feature (Gain: 1,528,782) due to hygroscopic aerosol growth effects. AOD ranks 9th due to 93% temporal sparsity, but provides a meaningful +0.13 R² improvement during clear-sky windows.

Key Findings

◈
Regional heterogeneity: Coastal cities (Thiruvananthapuram, Kolkata) exhibit 3× higher AOD sensitivity (~0.31) compared to inland cities, reflecting marine aerosol dynamics and sea-breeze patterns. Inland cities (Delhi, Lucknow, Nagpur) are dominated by Boundary Layer Height (~0.24–0.31), consistent with winter inversion trapping.
◈
Seasonal modulation: Winter PM2.5 concentrations are 2–3× higher than monsoon levels. Northern inland cities (Delhi, Lucknow) show median winter concentrations of 85–120 µg/m³ vs. 25–45 µg/m³ during monsoon — validating the need for winter-specific interventions like GRAP.
◈
AOD-PM2.5 correlation: Pearson r = 0.58 (p < 0.001) across 48,999 clear-sky observations, confirming physical utility. The relationship weakens at RH > 70% due to hygroscopic aerosol growth, justifying the RH × AOD interaction feature. Non-linearity in the relationship justifies gradient boosting over linear regression.
◈
Temporal validation: End-to-end time-series cross-validation with per-station 80/20 chronological splits yields holdout R² = 0.8543 for Model B, confirming generalization to unseen future periods without look-ahead bias.
Implications

A Tiered Monitoring Strategy

Rather than replacing ground infrastructure, this framework proposes a pragmatic, cost-graduated deployment matching monitoring modality to city capacity.

Tier 1
Delhi · Mumbai · Kolkata
Maintain CAAQMS + augment with satellite AI for spatial gap-filling
High-fidelity coverage with redundancy; satellite fills station downtime
Tier 2
Bhubaneswar · Lucknow · Nagpur
Satellite AI as primary; selective ground sensors in high-risk zones
85–90% cost reduction vs. full ground network; continuous baseline coverage
Tier 3
4,000+ smaller cities
Satellite AI only with periodic ground calibration campaigns
Economically feasible monitoring where ground infrastructure is prohibitive

Future Directions

  • All-weather AOD retrieval: Integrate microwave sensors or ML-based cloud correction to reduce the 93% temporal sparsity of optical AOD.
  • City-specific adaptation: Develop hierarchical or meta-learning frameworks enabling rapid deployment to new cities with minimal ground calibration.
  • Real-time deployment pipeline: Build automated ingestion → prediction → alert systems integrated with emission inventories for source attribution and GRAP interventions.
Data & Resources

Data Sources & External Links

All code and the compiled dataset are publicly available to ensure reproducibility. The following sources were used in this study.

MOSDAC — INSAT-3DS AOD ↗

ISRO's Meteorological & Oceanographic Satellite Data Archival Centre. Source of the INSAT-3DS Level-2 Gridded Aerosol Optical Depth (V02R00) product used in this study. 1,505 HDF5 granules retrieved via Selenium automation.

mosdac.gov.in
ERA5 — ECMWF Climate Data Store ↗

European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts reanalysis dataset at 0.25°×0.25° resolution. Boundary Layer Height, temperature, humidity, wind, and surface pressure fields used as model inputs.

cds.climate.copernicus.eu
CPCB — India Air Quality Data ↗

Central Pollution Control Board of India. Hourly PM2.5 observations from Continuous Ambient Air Quality Monitoring Stations (CAAQMS) across all nine study cities. Ground truth for model training and validation.

cpcb.nic.in
OpenAQ — Open Air Quality Platform ↗

Open-source platform aggregating real-time and historical air quality data from governments and research institutions worldwide, cited in the context of global monitoring infrastructure gaps.

openaq.org
XGBoost ↗

GPU-accelerated gradient boosting library used for both model configurations. tree_method='hist' with CUDA device acceleration. Handles sparse AOD data natively via default split learning.

xgboost.readthedocs.io
Optuna — Hyperparameter Optimization ↗

Automatic hyperparameter optimization framework used to tune XGBoost parameters (max_depth, learning_rate, subsample, colsample_bytree, gamma, reg_lambda, reg_alpha) by minimizing RMSE on the validation set.

optuna.org
SHAP — Model Interpretability ↗

SHapley Additive exPlanations used to interpret Model B predictions. Reveals that pm25_lag1 (mean |SHAP| = 49.23) contributes ~10× more than all other features, confirming statistical persistence learning.

shap.readthedocs.io
Natural Earth — Base Map Data ↗

Public domain map dataset used as the base cartographic layer for the study area visualization showing the nine Indian cities and their geographic context (coastal vs. inland classification).

naturalearthdata.com
